# 9 Keys — Mythology and Naming

The game's item names draw from Greek mythology, specifically from the myth of the Labyrinth of Crete. This document explains the source material and how each name connects to its in-game function.

## The Myth of the Labyrinth

King Minos of Crete kept the Minotaur — a creature half man, half bull — imprisoned in a vast, inescapable maze called the Labyrinth. The Labyrinth was designed by **Daedalus**, a master craftsman and inventor, specifically to be so complex that no one who entered could find their way out.

Athens was forced to send fourteen young men and women into the Labyrinth as tribute to be devoured by the Minotaur. The Athenian hero **Theseus** volunteered to go as one of the fourteen, intending to kill the Minotaur and end the tribute.

**Ariadne**, the daughter of King Minos, fell in love with Theseus. She gave him a ball of thread to unwind as he entered the Labyrinth so he could retrace his steps after slaying the Minotaur. Theseus killed the Minotaur, followed the thread back out, and escaped Crete with Ariadne.

Daedalus, having helped Ariadne (and by extension Theseus), was imprisoned by Minos. To escape, Daedalus crafted wings from feathers and wax for himself and his son **Icarus**. Daedalus warned Icarus not to fly too close to the sun. Icarus ignored the warning, the wax melted, and he fell into the sea and drowned. Daedalus escaped and lived, carrying the grief of his son's death.

## Argus Panoptes

**Argus Panoptes** ("Argus the All-Seeing") is from a separate myth. He was a giant with a hundred eyes — some accounts say they were spread across his entire body, others say he had eyes in the back of his head, but the consistent detail is that he could see everything. Not all of his eyes slept at once, so he was perpetually watchful.

The goddess Hera assigned Argus to guard Io, a woman Zeus had transformed into a cow to hide an affair. Zeus sent Hermes to rescue Io. Hermes lulled all of Argus's eyes to sleep with music and stories, then killed him. Hera honored Argus by placing his hundred eyes on the tail feathers of the peacock.

## How the Names Map to the Game

### Ariadne's Thread

**In myth:** A ball of thread that allowed Theseus to navigate the Labyrinth by marking where he had been, ensuring he could always find his way back.

**In game:** An item that indicates whether there are accessible unexplored spaces down a given path. Like the mythological thread, it solves the fundamental problem of navigating a maze — not by showing the answer, but by showing where you haven't yet looked.

**Why it fits:** The thread didn't tell Theseus where the Minotaur was. It told him where he'd been. The in-game version works the same way — it doesn't reveal the maze, it reveals where exploration remains.

### Argus's Eye

**In myth:** Argus Panoptes could see everything around him at all times. His defining trait was perception — not wisdom, not strength, but the ability to see what others could not.

**In game:** An upgradeable item that expands the player's visible area from 1x1 to 3x3, then 5x5, then 7x7. Each upgrade grants the player more of Argus's all-seeing nature. The vision is personal — only the player who holds the Eye benefits, just as Argus's sight was his alone.

**Why it fits:** Argus's power was purely perceptive. He didn't change the world around him; he simply saw more of it. The in-game Eye works identically — it doesn't alter the maze, it reveals more of it to the one who carries it. The upgrade progression (see more, see further) mirrors the myth's emphasis on ever-expanding awareness.

### The Labyrinth Itself

**In myth:** An inescapable maze designed by the greatest craftsman who ever lived, built specifically so that no one could navigate it unaided.

**In game:** A procedurally generated maze with gated progression, where the player must find nine keys to reach the end. The maze is always solvable but never obvious, and the optional items (Thread and Eye) serve the same role Ariadne's gifts served for Theseus — tools that make the impossible merely difficult.

### The Name "9 Keys"

The game's title does not reference Greek mythology. This is intentional. The mythological names live inside the game as discoveries — rewards for players who recognize them. The title communicates what the game *is* (a maze with nine keys to find) rather than what it *references*. A player who has never heard of Ariadne or Argus loses nothing; a player who has gains a layer of thematic resonance.
